# Interview 05 — Greg Lindqvist

**Persona:** Greg Lindqvist, 46. Lead agronomist at a regional farmer-owned cooperative in
east-central Illinois. Advises ~80 grower-members; the co-op sells seed, chemical, fertilizer,
and custom application. He is the "Kevin" that farmers like Dale rely on — the trusted channel
that also sells the product.
**Date:** 2026-06-13 · **Format:** video, ~45 min
**Recruited via:** co-op industry contact

---

**Founder:** Greg, you're the person several farmers I've talked to said they trust most. But
you also work for the co-op that sells the chemical. Help me understand how you hold both.

**Greg:** Yeah, people love to point at that conflict, and I get it. Here's the reality:
these are my neighbors and they're member-owners of this co-op — they literally own a piece
of it. If I sell a guy a program that fails, he's not just a lost sale, he's at the counter
Monday morning unhappy and he owns the place. My whole job is repeat trust. I'll talk a guy
*out* of a fungicide pass he doesn't need all the time, because being right keeps him for
twenty years. The independent consultants like to say we just push product. The good ones
among us don't last if we do.

**Founder:** That said — you do have volume targets, sales goals?

**Greg:** Of course. I'm not going to pretend otherwise. There's pressure to move product,
hit the season's numbers. So yeah, on the margin, am I more likely to reach for the program
that's a little heavier when it's a coin flip? I'm human. The structure pulls that direction
even when I'm trying to do right by the grower. That's the honest answer, and it's exactly
why independents exist.

**Founder:** How do you actually build a program for a grower? What's your process and your
tools?

**Greg:** I've got the co-op's system — it's an agronomy platform tied to our sales and
inventory, it does field records, recommendations, generates the order. It's decent for the
transaction. For the actual agronomic thinking — resistance, modes of action, what's working
across the territory — that's honestly in my head and a lot of experience. I'm managing 80
growers. I know which townships have PPO-resistant waterhemp because I've watched it spread
field to field for a decade. That knowledge isn't in any software. It's in me.

**Founder:** That's the thing I keep hearing — the knowledge is in people's heads. Is that a
problem for the co-op?

**Greg:** Huge problem, and nobody talks about it. I'm 46. We've got two agronomists here
pushing retirement. When they walk out the door, thirty years of "this field, this weed, this
history" walks out with them. We're trying to hire young agronomists and there aren't enough,
and the new ones don't have the territory knowledge yet. If something could capture and hold
that institutional knowledge — the resistance map of our whole trade area, field by field —
that's worth a lot to a co-op. More than to any individual farmer.

**Founder:** Say I built a tool that helped you manage resistance across your 80 growers —
flagged when a program was stacking modes of action, held the territory's resistance history,
made your younger agronomists nearly as good as you. Who buys that — you, or the co-op?

**Greg:** The co-op buys it. Individual agronomists don't have a software budget; the co-op
does. And the co-op has a real reason to want it — continuity, consistency across agronomists,
and frankly defensibility against the independents. If our programs got measurably better at
stewardship, that's a story we tell our members. The trick is it can't fight our sales system
— it's got to feed the order, not replace it. And it absolutely cannot publicly make us look
like we're overselling, or it's dead on arrival internally.

**Founder:** That's a real tension. A stewardship tool that says "spray less" inside a
company that sells chemical.

**Greg:** You've put your finger on it. That's the needle to thread. My honest take: position
it as "keep your chemistries working longer so you keep selling effective product for years"
rather than "sell less today." A resistant weed that's blown past all our chemistries is a
customer we can't help and a member we lose. Stewardship is actually long-term good business
for a co-op — but you have to sell it that way, not as degrowth. Lead with degrowth and every
co-op slams the door.

**Founder:** Where does the farmer fit in this? Do they care about the software at all?

**Greg:** Honestly? No. The farmer wants clean fields, a fair price, and to trust his
agronomist. He does not want another app and he does not care what's running behind my
recommendation. The relationship is the product to him. So if you're building this, the
farmer is the *beneficiary* but not the *buyer* and not the *user*. The buyer is the co-op or
the independent. The user is the agronomist. Get those three roles straight or you'll build
the wrong thing.

**Founder:** That's maybe the clearest thing anyone's said to me. Last question — the AI,
90%-less-chemical vision. From inside the channel, what happens to a co-op in that world?

**Greg:** [laughs] You're asking the guy whose revenue is chemical sales whether he wants to
sell 90% less chemical. But seriously — co-ops aren't dumb, we see it coming. The ones that
survive will shift from selling gallons to selling outcomes and services. Precision
application, biologicals, agronomic services, data. If you help us make that transition
instead of just blowing up our P&L, you've got a partner with relationships in every farm
county in America. Come at us as a disruptor trying to kill the channel, and the channel —
which is the only thing the farmer actually trusts — will bury you. We're the distribution.
Don't forget that.

**Founder:** Noted, loudly. Thank you, Greg.

**Greg:** Good luck. And whoever you build for — figure out who signs the check. It's not the
farmer.

### Interviewer notes
- The "conflicted channel" is **more nuanced than pure conflict**: member-ownership +
  repeat-trust partly self-corrects, but Greg admits the structure tilts toward heavier
  programs "on the margin." Confirms why independents exist.
- New, strong insight: **institutional knowledge is trapped in aging agronomists' heads**;
  retirements = looming loss for co-ops. A resistance/territory-history tool has *enterprise*
  (co-op) value beyond any single farm.
- Clean **role separation**: farmer = beneficiary (not buyer, not user); agronomist = user;
  co-op or independent = buyer/check-signer. This is the sharpest answer to
  `who-is-the-customer` in the panel.
- Critical GTM/positioning tension: a "spray less" tool inside a chemical-selling co-op.
  Threading the needle = **"make your chemistries last longer"** (long-term business) NOT
  "degrowth." Lead with degrowth → door slams.
- Channel power warning: co-ops are the trusted distribution into every farm county; partner
  with the channel or it buries you. Mirrors Dale's "you're not getting around Kevin."
- Even the channel sees the transition coming: gallons → outcomes/services. Possible wedge:
  help co-ops make that shift.
