# Customer Discovery Synthesis
*Generated: 2026-06-14 | Interviews analyzed: 5*

> **Provenance:** Synthesized from the five illustrative sample transcripts in this folder
> (see README). Treat patterns as hypotheses to confirm with real buyers before relying on
> them for an actual build, raise, or pitch.

## TL;DR
Across the panel, the loudest and most consistent signal is that **the farmer is the
beneficiary but not the buyer or the user** — the agronomist is the user, and the co-op or
independent consultant signs the check. The core unmet pain is that **resistance knowledge
lives in people's heads and spreadsheets**, and it's getting worse as chemistries fail and
senior agronomists retire. But the killer risk is structural: a tool whose explicit value is
"use less chemical" has to be sold to a channel whose revenue *is* chemical — so the riskiest
thing to confirm is that a willing, well-funded buyer actually exists. Next move: stop
validating the problem (it's real) and start validating the *payer*.

## Patterns (appeared in 3+ interviews)

### Pattern 1: The agronomist is the decision-maker; the farmer "buys the person," not software
**Frequency:** 5/5 interviews
**The problem:** Spray decisions aren't made by farmers reading dashboards — they're made by
a trusted agronomist (independent or co-op) and executed on relationship. Farmers actively
*don't want* another app and don't care what's behind the recommendation.
**What they do today:** Farmers call their agronomist (Kevin/Maria/Greg/a consultant). The
agronomist holds the plan; the farmer trusts and executes.
**Best quote:** "The grower's never going to buy software; the grower buys *me*. I'm the
customer." — Maria, independent CCA

### Pattern 2: The resistance treadmill is real, worsening, and the chemistry pipeline is dry
**Frequency:** 5/5 interviews
**The problem:** Glyphosate is long dead on waterhemp/Palmer; PPOs are failing; growers lean
on Group 15s with nothing new coming. Everyone is managing decline, not winning.
**What they do today:** Layer residuals, rotate modes of action, spray more, and — at the
extreme — hire hand-weeding crews with hoes.
**Best quote:** "The honest dream isn't 90% less — it's not going *backwards*. We're losing
chemistries faster than we're getting new ones." — Coleman, 9,000-ac Delta

### Pattern 3: The resistance "brain" is trapped in human memory + duct-taped spreadsheets
**Frequency:** 4/5 interviews (Maria, Tyler, Greg explicit; Coleman implicit via consultant records)
**The problem:** Nobody has a system that holds field-by-field resistance history and flags
when a program stacks modes of action wrong. Existing software (FieldView, co-op platforms)
maps and transacts but doesn't *think about resistance*.
**What they do today:** Color-coded spreadsheets (Maria), a Google Sheet + memory (Tyler),
"it's in my head and thirty years of experience" (Greg).
**Best quote:** "FieldView maps yield beautifully. It does not tell me 'you've run Group 14
three passes in a row, you're cooking yourself.'" — Tyler, tech-forward farmer

### Pattern 4: Ag software dies on data entry; it has to be a load-bearing weekly habit
**Frequency:** 3/5 interviews (Tyler explicit; Dale's FieldView churn; Greg's "can't fight the order")
**The problem:** Tools that require manual data entry or just make "pretty maps" get
abandoned. Only software you open weekly because it's load-bearing survives.
**What they do today:** Sign up, set up, then quietly stop opening it.
**Best quote:** "Pretty maps don't pay the loan." — Dale, Iowa corn/soy

## Jobs to be done

- **Agronomist:** When I'm managing dozens of growers across thousands of acres, I want to
  hold each field's resistance history and catch when a program stacks modes of action wrong,
  so I can serve more acres without my quality or reputation slipping.
- **Farmer:** When in-season pressure hits, I want clean fields *now* for the least money, so
  I can protect this year's margin — without gambling my whole year on an unproven tool.
- **Co-op:** When my senior agronomists retire, I want to capture their territory knowledge
  and keep program quality consistent across agronomists, so I can defend against independents
  and keep my members.
- **Large operator:** When I have a narrow weather/regulatory window and thousands of acres, I
  want the application sequence that protects the most yield and stays inside the label, so I
  don't lose a good plan to logistics — and clean records if a drift complaint comes.

## Surprises (things you did not expect to hear)

- **The conflict of interest is known and tolerated.** Farmers know the co-op profits from
  what it sells and trust it anyway — local accountability ("he's gotta look me in the eye at
  church") substitutes for structural independence. The conflict is a feature of the
  relationship, not a dealbreaker.
- **The biggest enterprise value may be knowledge capture, not agronomy.** Greg's strongest
  pain wasn't resistance per se — it was thirty years of territory knowledge walking out the
  door at retirement with no system to hold it. That reframes the product as *institutional
  memory for co-ops*, not just a decision tool.
- **At scale, logistics beats agronomy.** Coleman already has the agronomy solved by a
  consultant; his unmet need is orchestrating crews across 9,000 acres inside shrinking
  weather/dicamba windows. Same disease, different symptom.
- **You may have to sell "spray less" as "spray effective longer."** The only positioning that
  survives inside a chemical-selling co-op is stewardship-as-long-term-business, never degrowth.

## The riskiest assumption

**That a willing, well-funded economic buyer exists for software whose explicit value is
reducing chemical use.** The panel agrees the farmer won't pay; that leaves the co-op (whose
revenue *is* chemical — structural conflict) and the independent consultant (right incentive,
acute pain, but a small per-seat budget and a fragmented market of a few thousand).

**Why it is risky:** The problem being real does not mean someone will pay to solve it. The
party with the most aligned incentive (independents) has the least money; the party with the
budget (co-ops) has a revenue model the product partially undermines. If neither converts,
there is no business even with a great product.

**How to test it:** In the next interviews, stop confirming the pain and instead get to price
and budget. Ask independents: "What do you pay per year for software today, and would you pay
$X/seat or $Y/acre for this?" Ask co-ops: "Who owns the software budget, what's the approval
process, and would a stewardship tool clear it given it may reduce volume?" A verbal "I'd try
it" is not a buyer; a budget line and an approver is.

## What you still do not know

- [ ] What do independent CCAs actually pay for software today, and what's their willingness
      to pay (per-seat vs per-acre)?
- [ ] Inside a co-op, who owns the software budget and would a "spray less" tool survive
      internal approval?
- [ ] Is the real wedge resistance *decisioning*, logistics/execution, or knowledge capture?
      (Three different products — the panel split.)
- [ ] How big and reachable is the independent-consultant market really? (Few thousand? How
      do you sell to them?)
- [ ] Would auto-ingesting as-applied data (the "no data entry" requirement) actually be
      technically possible across the equipment/software stack farmers run?
- [ ] Does any of this need to be different by crop and region (Corn Belt corn/soy vs Delta
      cotton)? The panel suggests yes.

## Recommended next interviews

Talk to **5–8 economic buyers, not problem-havers**: 3–4 independent crop consultants (probe
willingness-to-pay and per-seat economics) and 2–3 co-op managers or agronomy-division leads
(probe budget ownership and whether a stewardship tool clears internal approval). Prioritize
one question above all: **"Walk me through the last piece of software your business bought —
who decided, what did it cost, and why that one?"** That reveals the real buying process the
problem interviews can't.
