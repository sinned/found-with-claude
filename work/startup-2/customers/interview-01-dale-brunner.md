# Interview 01 — Dale Brunner

**Persona:** Dale Brunner, 58. Third-generation farmer near Carroll, west-central Iowa.
~3,200 acres, corn/soybean rotation. Conventional. Banks and buys most inputs through the
local co-op he's used for 30 years.
**Date:** 2026-06-09 · **Format:** phone, ~35 min
**Recruited via:** cold call referred by a seed dealer

---

**Founder:** Thanks for taking the time, Dale. I'm trying to learn how farms like yours
actually make decisions about spraying — herbicides, pesticides. Not selling anything, just
trying to understand the day-to-day. Can you walk me through a normal season?

**Dale:** Sure. Well, it starts before the season really. Fall, we'll do a burndown on some
ground. Then spring it's pre-emerge before planting, and post-emerge after the beans and
corn come up. Then we watch. If the waterhemp gets away from us, we're back in the field.

**Founder:** Waterhemp's the main one you fight?

**Dale:** Waterhemp's the devil. Used to be we'd hit it with glyphosate and that was that.
Roundup Ready, spray it, done, go fishing. That's been over for, oh, ten years now. The
glyphosate just doesn't touch it anymore. Now it's layered residuals, multiple modes of
action, and you still get escapes. My dad would not believe what we spend now.

**Founder:** What do you spend, roughly, if you don't mind?

**Dale:** On chemical? Beans, you're talking — between the burndown, the pre, the post,
sometimes a second post — north of 70, 80 bucks an acre some years. Corn's a little less.
It adds up quick on 3,200 acres. And that's if the weather cooperates. If I get a flush
after a rain and have to go again, there goes the margin.

**Founder:** So when you decide what to put in the tank — the actual products and rates —
how does that decision get made?

**Dale:** Honestly? I sit down with Kevin at the co-op. He's my agronomist there, been
working with him fifteen years. He pulls up my fields, we talk about what I had last year,
what's coming, and he builds me a program. I trust Kevin. He's not gonna steer me wrong.

**Founder:** Does it ever cross your mind that the co-op is also the one selling you the
chemical?

**Dale:** [laughs] Course it does. I'm not stupid. Kevin's gotta sell product, that's his
job. But here's the thing — if Kevin sells me something that doesn't work, I'm gonna be in
his office raising hell, and he knows it. He's gotta look me in the eye at church. That
relationship keeps him honest more than you'd think. Could I shave some cost shopping around?
Probably. But Kevin's been there at 9 at night when I had a question. That's worth something.

**Founder:** That makes sense. Let me ask — if there were a piece of software, an app or a
service, that looked at your fields and told you "here's the program that'll use the least
chemical and slow down resistance" — would you use something like that?

**Dale:** Eh. [pause] I mean, maybe. Depends. We tried that FieldView deal — the Climate
thing. Bought it, my son set it all up, we used it hard for about a year and then it kind of
just... we stopped opening it. It makes pretty maps. It told me stuff I mostly already knew.

**Founder:** What would've made you keep using it?

**Dale:** If it actually saved me money I could see. Or did something I couldn't do myself.
Pretty maps don't pay the loan. And honestly — at the end of the day, I'm still gonna call
Kevin. I'm not gonna spray a program some app told me to if Kevin says it won't hold. The
app's not standing in my field in August when the waterhemp's over my head.

**Founder:** So even if the app's recommendation was good, you'd want Kevin to bless it.

**Dale:** Hundred percent. It's my whole year riding on it. You don't gamble that on a
computer you've known for six months over a guy you've known for fifteen years.

**Founder:** What if the software was something Kevin used — so it made him better, faster —
rather than something that replaced him?

**Dale:** Oh. Yeah, that's different. If Kevin's got a better tool, great, I don't even need
to know about it. That's between him and the co-op. I just want the program that works for
the least money. I don't care what software's behind it.

**Founder:** On resistance specifically — slowing it down so your chemicals keep working
longer — is that something you actively think about, or is it more "deal with it when it
shows up"?

**Dale:** I'd love to tell you I'm playing the long game. Truth is, in-season I'm trying to
keep fields clean this year. Resistance is a next-year problem, and there's always a fire
this year. I know rotating modes of action matters. I know I should. But when the waterhemp's
exploding and rain's coming, I'm reaching for what knocks it down now. That's just honest.

**Founder:** That's really helpful. Last thing — if a tool could prove it'd cut your chemical
bill 20% without losing yield, what would you need to see to believe it?

**Dale:** My neighbor doing it first. [laughs] I'm serious. Show me Larry down the road cut
his bill and his beans looked just as good as mine. I'll believe Larry's field before I
believe any salesman or any app. We're show-me people out here. Nobody wants to be the guy
who tried the new thing and lost the farm.

**Founder:** Understood. Thank you, Dale, this was exactly what I needed.

**Dale:** Yep. Good luck to you. And whatever you build — make it talk to the co-op guys.
You're not getting around Kevin.

---

### Interviewer notes
- Decision is **relationship-mediated**: the retailer agronomist (Kevin) is the real
  decision-maker, not Dale directly. Conflict-of-interest is *known and tolerated* —
  trust + accountability (church, local) substitutes for independence.
- FieldView churned: "pretty maps don't pay the loan." Bar for software = visible $ saved
  or do-something-I-can't.
- Resistance = "next-year problem"; this-year fire always wins. Long-game framing won't sell.
- Strong signal toward selling *to/through the agronomist*, not direct-to-farmer.
- Social proof ("my neighbor did it") > any pitch.
