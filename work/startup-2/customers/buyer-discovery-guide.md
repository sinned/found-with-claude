# Buyer-Discovery Interview Guide
*Created: 2026-06-14 | For: startup-2 (reduced-chemical ag)*

## Purpose

The problem is not the open question anymore — the **buyer** is. This guide is built to
kill or confirm the riskiest assumption:

> **A willing, well-funded economic buyer exists for software whose explicit value is
> reducing chemical use.** (`willing-buyer-exists-for-spray-less-software`)

These are NOT problem-discovery interviews. You already believe the problem. The job here is
to find a real price, a real budget owner, and a real approval path. A verbal "I'd try it" is
a failure, not a success — push past enthusiasm to commitment.

## Who to talk to (5–8 people)

| Count | Who | Why |
|-------|-----|-----|
| 3–4 | Independent crop consultants / CCAs (fee-for-service) | Most aligned incentive; test per-seat WTP and how they actually buy tools |
| 2–3 | Co-op agronomy-division managers or leads | Hold the budget; test whether a "spray less" tool survives internal approval |
| 1 (optional) | An ag-software buyer who recently churned a tool | Learn why tools die in this market |

How to reach them: state/regional CCA association directories, co-op corporate contacts,
ag-tech newsletter communities, cotton/corn grower associations, warm intros from any farmer
you meet. Aim for the person who *signs the check*, not just the person with the pain.

## Ground rules

- **Don't pitch.** The moment you pitch, they get polite and the data dies. Describe the
  concept in one neutral sentence only when asked, then go back to questions.
- **Past behavior > future intent.** "What did you buy last?" beats "Would you buy this?"
- **Get to a number.** If you leave without a price, budget range, or approver name, the
  interview underdelivered.
- **Silence is a tool.** After they answer a money question, wait. They'll add the real reason.

## Section 1 — How they buy software today (the core of this guide)

1. Walk me through the **last piece of software your business bought**. What was it?
2. Who decided? Who else had to sign off? How long did it take?
3. What did it cost — per seat, per acre, per year? Was that a hard or easy yes?
4. What made *that* one worth paying for when so many ag tools get abandoned?
5. What's the last tool you **stopped paying for**, and why?
6. (Co-op) Who owns the software/technology budget here? What's the approval threshold where
   it stops being your call and goes up the chain?
7. (Independent) What do you spend per year on software today, all-in? Out of your own pocket?

## Section 2 — Value framing & willingness to pay

8. If a tool could let you serve **30% more acres without dropping quality** (independent) /
   **keep program quality consistent as senior agronomists retire** (co-op) — what would that
   be worth to you per year?
9. Would you rather pay per seat, per acre, or as a flat subscription? Why?
10. What would it have to *do in week one* for you to keep paying in month three?
11. (Co-op) If the tool's recommendation sometimes means **selling less chemical this season**,
    does that change whether you'd buy it? Who internally would object?
12. What's a price that would feel obviously worth it? A price that would feel insulting-cheap
    (i.e., "that can't be any good")?

## Section 3 — Which product? (resolve the three-way value split)

The synthesis split on where value lives. Listen for which one *they* light up on:

13. If you could wave a wand and fix **one** of these, which and why:
    (a) a resistance "brain" that flags mode-of-action stacking field-by-field,
    (b) logistics/sequencing across acres inside weather & label windows,
    (c) capturing your best agronomists' territory knowledge before they retire?
14. Walk me through the last time one of those three actually cost you money or a customer.
15. Which of the three would you pay for *first*?

## Section 4 — Channel & adoption reality

16. (Independent) Would you put your name/liability on a recommendation a tool generated? What
    would the tool have to show you for that to be OK?
17. How does data get into your current tools — do you type it, or does it flow in
    automatically? What's the most painful data entry in your week?
18. (Co-op) How would a tool like this have to relate to your existing sales/order system to be
    allowed in the door?
19. If this existed and worked, how would you expect to first hear about it — and from whom
    would you actually believe it?

## Section 5 — The commitment test (do this every time)

20. If I built a rough version for next season, would you be willing to **pay a pilot fee**
    (even small) to be a first user? — *Watch the reaction to "pay," not "be a first user."*
21. Who else exactly like you should I talk to? (A real referral is a real signal.)
22. Can I come back to you in 60 days with a prototype? (A yes with a calendar slot > a yes.)

## How to score each interview (fill this after every call)

- [ ] Got a concrete price/budget number? (Y/N) → ____
- [ ] Identified the actual check-signer? (Y/N) → ____
- [ ] They named which of the 3 products matters most → ____
- [ ] Willing to pay a pilot fee? (this is the real signal) → ____
- [ ] Gave a warm referral or a calendar follow-up? → ____

**Verdict per interview:** real buyer / interested non-buyer / not the buyer

## What confirms vs. kills the assumption

**Confirmed if:** ≥2 buyers name a real price + budget path AND ≥1 will pay a pilot fee.
**Killed if:** everyone is enthusiastic but no budget/approver materializes, OR co-ops
consistently say a "spray less" tool can't clear internal approval AND independents won't pay
enough to sustain a business. Either way, you'll know what to build (or not) next.
