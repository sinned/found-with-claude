# Interview 02 — Maria Olvera

**Persona:** Maria Olvera, 41. Independent Certified Crop Adviser (CCA). Runs her own
consulting practice in south-central Nebraska. ~25 grower clients, ~60,000 acres total under
advisement, mostly corn, soybean, some seed corn and popcorn. Charges per-acre retainer;
sells no product. Scouts fields, writes recommendations.
**Date:** 2026-06-10 · **Format:** video, ~45 min
**Recruited via:** state CCA association directory

---

**Founder:** Maria, thanks. You're independent — you don't sell inputs, right? Just advice?

**Maria:** Correct. I'm fee-for-service. My growers pay me a per-acre fee — it ranges, call
it $8 to $14 an acre depending on the crop and how intensive the scouting is. I write the
recommendations; they buy the product wherever they want. That independence is the whole
value proposition. I'm the person in the room who doesn't make a dime off what's in the tank.

**Founder:** How's that different from what a co-op agronomist does?

**Maria:** The co-op guy is good — a lot of them are excellent — but his paycheck depends on
moving product. There's nothing evil about it, it's just the structure. When I tell a grower
"you don't need that fungicide pass this year, skip it," that's money out of a co-op's pocket
and money in my client's pocket. That's why people hire me. I save them more than my fee.

**Founder:** So walk me through how you actually build a spray recommendation.

**Maria:** It's a mix of art and data. I'm in the fields every week scouting — physically
walking, counting, looking at weed pressure, disease, insects. I know each field's history,
the resistance profile, what we ran last year. I pull weather, I look at the residual
chemistries and how they're holding. Then I write a program: what, what rate, what timing,
what to tank-mix, and critically — what modes of action, so we're not hammering the same
site of action every pass and breeding resistance.

**Founder:** The resistance piece — how much of your job is that?

**Maria:** It's the part I lose sleep over. Honestly it's the central problem of modern weed
management. We've burned through chemistries. Glyphosate's done on waterhemp and Palmer.
The PPO inhibitors are getting shaky. Group 15s we're leaning on hard. There aren't new
modes of action coming — the pipeline's basically dry, has been for years. So my job is
stewardship: make what we have last. Every grower who oversprays one mode of action is
borrowing against everyone's future.

**Founder:** That's a vivid way to put it. Here's a question I keep coming back to — when you
make a recommendation, do growers actually follow it?

**Maria:** [laughs] The eternal question. The good ones do, mostly. But there's always
slippage. I'll write a program with two effective residuals and a grower will call the co-op,
the co-op will say "we're out of that, here's a substitute," and now my resistance plan has a
hole in it and I find out in August. Or they'll cut a rate to save money and I'm fighting
escapes. Compliance is maybe 70, 80% on a good client, less on a price-sensitive one.

**Founder:** What tools do you use to do all this? Software, spreadsheets?

**Maria:** [groans] You're going to laugh. It's a mess. I've got a scouting app for field
notes. I've got spreadsheets for each grower's program. I've got the chemical labels
bookmarked. I've got a separate thing for resistance tracking that's honestly just my own
spreadsheet with color coding. I'm stitching it together in my head. The hard part isn't any
one piece — it's keeping the whole picture coherent across 25 growers and 60,000 acres in my
head while I'm standing in a field with mud on my boots.

**Founder:** If a tool could hold that picture for you — every field's resistance history,
flag when you're stacking modes of action wrong, suggest a stewardship-optimal program you
could then edit — would that be valuable?

**Maria:** Genuinely? Yes. That's the thing I don't have. Not a tool that tells the *grower*
what to do — they won't listen to an app, they listen to me. A tool that makes *me* faster
and catches my blind spots, so I can take on more acres without dropping quality. Right now
I'm capacity-capped. I physically can't take more growers because I can't hold more in my
head. If software lifted that ceiling, that's real money for me.

**Founder:** So you'd pay for it — as the agronomist — versus the grower paying.

**Maria:** I'd pay for it if it let me serve more acres or charge more for better service.
The grower's never going to buy software; the grower buys *me*. I'm the customer. There are a
few thousand independent CCAs like me in the Corn Belt, plus all the retail agronomists.
We're the ones drowning in this complexity. The grower just wants clean fields and a smaller
bill.

**Founder:** What would make you NOT buy it?

**Maria:** If it tried to replace my judgment instead of augmenting it. If it spits out a
recommendation I can't see the reasoning behind, I won't put my name on it — it's my
liability and my reputation. And if it can't handle the real-world mess — product
substitutions, weather blowing up timing, a grower who ignored me last pass — then it's a
toy. The field doesn't care about the optimal plan; it cares about the plan that survived
contact with reality.

**Founder:** Last one. The big vision people talk about is cutting chemical use way down —
like 90% — with AI and precision. From where you sit, real or hype?

**Maria:** Both. The precision sprayers, the see-and-spray stuff — that's real and it's
coming, I've seen it cut herbicide on fallow ground dramatically. But 90% across the board
is a slide-deck number. In a standing crop, in season, with resistance pressure? You're not
cutting 90% without losing the farm. You might cut 30, 40% with smart stewardship plus
precision application, and honestly that would be revolutionary. Don't oversell it. Farmers
can smell hype from a mile off, and so can I.

**Founder:** That's incredibly helpful, Maria. Thank you.

**Maria:** Anytime. Build the thing for the agronomist. We're the leverage point. Nobody's
building good software for us.

### Interviewer notes
- **Strongest "who is the customer" signal so far: the independent agronomist.** "The grower
  buys *me*." She has budget, acute pain (capacity cap), and willingness to pay if it lifts
  her acre ceiling.
- Core pain = **holding the whole resistance picture coherent across many fields/growers**.
  Current stack = duct-taped apps + spreadsheets + memory.
- Hard requirements: explainable recommendations (liability/reputation), handles real-world
  mess (substitutions, weather, non-compliance). "Survived contact with reality."
- Confirms resistance pipeline is dry; stewardship framing resonates with *her* (not with
  Dale the farmer).
- Tempers the 90% thesis: realistic = 30–40% with stewardship + precision. Flags hype risk.
- Compliance with recommendations is only ~70–80% — adoption/behavior-change risk is real
  even when the advisor is trusted.
