# Interview 03 — Tyler Hoffmann

**Persona:** Tyler Hoffmann, 34. Farms ~1,800 acres corn/soybean near Fairmont, southern
Minnesota, with his dad and uncle. The "tech guy" of the operation. Runs Climate FieldView,
auto-steer/RTK, bought a used John Deere See & Spray retrofit two seasons ago. Active on
ag Twitter/X and a couple of farmer Discords.
**Date:** 2026-06-11 · **Format:** video, ~40 min
**Recruited via:** referral from an ag-tech newsletter community

---

**Founder:** Tyler, you're the rare farmer who actually likes the tech. Tell me what you're
running.

**Tyler:** [laughs] My dad calls me the IT department. Yeah — FieldView for data, RTK
guidance on everything, and the big one, we put a See & Spray retrofit on our sprayer two
years ago. Targeted spray, green-on-brown and now some green-on-green. That's the one that
actually changed our economics, not just made dashboards.

**Founder:** Tell me about the See & Spray numbers. Real savings or marketing?

**Tyler:** Real, but with an asterisk. On a burndown or fallow pass — green-on-brown, you're
only hitting actual weeds on bare ground — we're cutting herbicide volume like 60, 70%. No
joke. That pencils out fast. Green-on-green, in a standing soybean crop where it's trying to
tell crop from weed, it's more like 20-30% and it's fussier, misses some, you still need a
broadcast residual underneath. So the headline number depends a lot on which pass.

**Founder:** So the precision hardware works but it's not magic across the board.

**Tyler:** Right. And it's expensive. We bought used and it was still a chunk. New off the
dealer lot, a lot of guys my dad's age look at the price and just laugh. The payback's real
on our acres but you've gotta run enough acres and believe the savings. A 600-acre guy?
Tough sell.

**Founder:** What about the resistance problem — waterhemp, all that. Does the tech help
there?

**Tyler:** That's actually where I get frustrated. The hardware sprays precisely, but it
doesn't *think* about resistance. It'll happily nuke the same weeds with the same mode of
action every pass if that's what's in the tank. The "what to spray and how to rotate
chemistries" decision — that's still on me and our agronomist. The robot's a great trigger
finger, it's not a brain.

**Founder:** Say more about that gap. Who decides the actual chemistry?

**Tyler:** We use a retail agronomist for the program but I push back on him a lot because I
read a ton. I track our own resistance — like I know which fields have PPO-resistant
waterhemp because we've watched it. But I'm tracking it in, embarrassingly, a Google Sheet
and my memory. FieldView doesn't really do resistance management. It maps yield and
as-applied beautifully. It does not tell me "hey, you've run Group 14 three passes in a row
on this field, you're cooking yourself."

**Founder:** If something did do that — tracked your resistance by field, warned you when
your program was stacking wrong, suggested a rotation — would you use it?

**Tyler:** Yeah, I'd at least try it. I try everything. But — and this is the honest part —
"I'd try it" and "I'd pay for it every year" are different. I've got a graveyard of ag apps I
signed up for and abandoned. The ones that stick are the ones I open every single week
because they're load-bearing. FieldView stuck because of as-applied and yield maps at
harvest. A resistance tool would have to become a weekly habit, not a thing I check in March
and forget.

**Founder:** What makes something become a weekly habit for you?

**Tyler:** It has to be where the action is. If I have to go to a separate app and type stuff
in, it dies. If it rode along with what I already do — pulled my as-applied automatically,
knew what I sprayed without me entering it, and pinged me *before* the next pass with "here's
the resistance-smart move" — that's sticky. The data entry is the killer. Every farmer app
dies on data entry. We're not gonna sit there typing field records at night.

**Founder:** That's a great point. On the customer question — would you buy this yourself, or
would it come through your agronomist or the equipment dealer?

**Tyler:** Me, probably, because I'm a nerd. But I'm not typical. Most guys my dad's age,
it'd have to come through someone they trust — the agronomist or the John Deere dealer.
Honestly if it just showed up inside the John Deere Operations Center or FieldView as a
feature, that's the path of least resistance. Standalone app, you're fighting for a spot on
the home screen against the weather app and the grain markets. Tough real estate.

**Founder:** The grand vision — 90% less chemical with AI. You're closer to it than most.
Buy it?

**Tyler:** 90% is two things smushed together. On targeted application for certain passes,
sure, you can get huge cuts — I've seen it on my own farm. But 90% of *total* chemical
across a whole resistant-weed season? Not without something replacing the chemistry itself —
the biologicals, or weeds just not being there. The application tech caps out. The next leg
has to come from biology or genetics, and that's not my lane, that's a science problem. I
just drive the sprayer better.

**Founder:** This was gold, Tyler. Thank you.

**Tyler:** For sure. If you build the resistance brain that rides inside FieldView or Ops
Center and doesn't make me type — put me on the beta. I mean it.

  ~20-30% green-on-green. Confirms #2 (precision) value and its ceiling.

  *decisioning* is the unserved gap on top of existing precision hardware. Validates #1.
- Killer adoption insight: **"every farmer app dies on data entry"** and "load-bearing
  weekly habit" test. Auto-ingest as-applied data; warn *before* the pass.
- Distribution: ride inside John Deere Operations Center / FieldView, not standalone app.
  Even the tech-forward farmer says standalone real estate is brutal.
- Even an early adopter says direct-to-farmer only works for nerds; mainstream comes through
  agronomist or dealer. Reinforces interview-01/02.
- Confirms 90% is application + biology/genetics combined, not application alone.
