# Interview 04 — Coleman Reyes

**Persona:** Coleman Reyes, 49. Large operation, ~9,000 acres, eastern Arkansas / Mississippi
Delta. Cotton, corn, some soybeans and rice. Runs a real management team — a farm manager,
spray crews, seasonal labor. Has lived the Palmer amaranth and dicamba-drift wars firsthand.
**Date:** 2026-06-12 · **Format:** phone, ~50 min
**Recruited via:** introduction through a cotton grower association contact

---

**Founder:** Coleman, appreciate it. You farm at a scale most people can't picture. What
keeps you up at night on the pest and weed side?

**Coleman:** Pigweed. Palmer amaranth. I could talk to you for an hour just on that. That
weed nearly broke cotton in the Delta. One plant makes a half million seeds. It grows two
inches a day in July heat. And it's resistant to damn near everything — glyphosate's been
useless for fifteen years, it shrugged off the PPOs, and we're watching it work on the next
ones. When I started we sprayed Roundup and went home. Now I've got hand-weeding crews —
actual people with hoes — walking cotton in 2026. That's medieval. And it's expensive.

**Founder:** Hand crews — what does that cost you?

**Coleman:** Depends on the year and how bad the escape is, but in a bad field I'm spending
real money per acre just on people walking with hoes, on top of a chemical program that's
already three, four times what it was. You add it up across 9,000 acres and the weed control
line item is one of the biggest numbers on my whole operation. And labor's getting harder to
find every year.

**Founder:** And the dicamba situation — you lived through that?

**Coleman:** [exhales] Don't get me started. We went to dicamba-tolerant cotton and beans to
get another tool on the pigweed. Then half the Delta's spraying dicamba, it volatilizes, it
moves, and now your neighbor's non-tolerant beans are cupped up and somebody's calling a
lawyer. I've been on both sides of that — worried about my own drift and worried about
getting hit. The regulators have been on-again off-again with the label. It's chaos. So now
there's a chemistry that works but I'm scared to use it because of liability and the
shrinking application window. That's the world we're in.

**Founder:** That's a brutal picture. When you build your weed program at this scale, who's
making the call — you, a manager, an agronomist?

**Coleman:** At my size it's a team. I've got a farm manager and we use a consultant — an
independent guy, we pay him, he scouts and writes our programs. I don't let the chemical
dealer write my program; at my scale the conflict's too big, the dollars are too big. The
independent consultant earns his fee ten times over. But the execution — getting the right
product on the right field in the right window with my own sprayers and crews — that's a
logistics nightmare that's all on us.

**Founder:** So at scale you've already got the independent advisor. What's still broken?

**Coleman:** Execution and timing. The plan is one thing; pulling it off across 9,000 acres
with weather windows closing is another. I might have the perfect resistance-rotation plan on
paper, but if I can't cover the acres before the pigweed's too big or the rain comes or the
dicamba window slams shut, the plan's worthless. And tracking what actually went where — so
next year we know the resistance history field by field — it's spotty. We're better than most
and it's still held together with the consultant's records and our spray logs that don't
always match.

**Founder:** If you had a tool that tied it together — resistance history by field, optimized
which fields to hit in what order given the weather windows, kept the as-applied record clean
— would that move the needle for you?

**Coleman:** At my scale? The logistics optimization might be worth more to me than the
agronomy, honestly. The agronomy I've got covered with my consultant. But the "you've got
three days of weather and 4,000 acres that need a pass, here's the sequence that protects the
most yield and stays inside the dicamba label" — that's a real problem nobody's solved well.
And clean records that hold up if I ever get a drift complaint? That's worth money just for
the liability protection.

**Founder:** Interesting — so the value's different for you than for a smaller farmer.

**Coleman:** Completely. The 1,500-acre guy, his problem is he can't afford a precision
sprayer and he's price-shopping chemical. My problem is orchestrating a small army across a
huge footprint inside shrinking windows, with lawyers and regulators watching. Same disease,
different symptoms. Don't build one thing and think it fits both of us.

**Founder:** The 90%-less-chemical vision — from the Delta, where pigweed is this bad, does
that sound real to you?

**Coleman:** In the Delta? With Palmer? [laughs darkly] I'd settle for keeping what works
working. The honest dream isn't 90% less — it's not going *backwards*. We're losing
chemistries faster than we're getting new ones. If somebody could give me one new mode of
action that pigweed isn't resistant to, I'd pay anything. The precision sprayers help on
volume, the biologicals might help someday, but down here the existential thing is the
resistance treadmill itself. We're running out of road.

**Founder:** That's powerful, Coleman. Anything you'd tell someone trying to build for this?

**Coleman:** Come stand in a Delta cotton field in August before you build anything. The
people in conference rooms drawing 90% on slides have never had pigweed over their head with
a hoe crew costing them a fortune. The problem's real. The Silicon Valley version of the
problem usually isn't.

**Founder:** Fair. Thank you, Coleman. I'd take you up on the field visit.

**Coleman:** Door's open. Bring boots.

### Interviewer notes
- Extreme version of the **resistance treadmill**: Palmer amaranth resistant to nearly
  everything; hand-weeding crews in 2026; "running out of road." Highest pain in the panel.
- Dicamba = vivid example of "chemistry that works but is too risky/regulated to use freely"
  (drift, liability, shrinking window). New angle: **liability + recordkeeping** as a value driver.
- At scale, the customer **already has an independent consultant** — so the agronomy decision
  layer is *less* novel for him. His unmet need is **execution/logistics optimization** under
  weather + regulatory windows, and **clean as-applied records** for liability.
- Strong warning: **don't build one product for both small and large farms** — same disease,
  different symptoms. Segment deliberately.
- Tempers the 90% thesis hard from the high-pressure end: "I'd settle for not going
  backwards." The existential need is *new modes of action / replacing chemistry*, which is
  the biology/genetics lane (#3/#4), not software.
- "Come stand in a field before you build" — founder-fit / domain-credibility warning.
