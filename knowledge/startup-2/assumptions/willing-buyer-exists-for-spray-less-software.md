# A willing, well-funded economic buyer exists for software whose explicit value is reducing chemical use

**Validation status:** unvalidated
The panel agrees the problem (resistance treadmill, knowledge trapped in heads) is real and
the farmer won't pay for software. That leaves two candidate buyers, each with a flaw: the
co-op has budget but a revenue model the product undermines (sells chemical), and the
independent consultant has aligned incentive and acute pain but a small per-seat budget in a
fragmented market. A real problem does not guarantee a payer.

**What "validated" looks like:** An independent names a per-seat/per-acre price they'd pay and
points to comparable software they already buy; OR a co-op identifies a budget owner and a
plausible internal approval path for a stewardship tool despite its volume impact.
**What "invalidated" looks like:** Independents love it but won't pay enough to build a
business; co-ops kill it in approval because it reduces chemical sales; everyone says "great
idea" with no budget behind it.
**How we'll test it:** Next 5–8 interviews target economic buyers, not problem-havers. Ask:
"Walk me through the last software your business bought — who decided, what did it cost, why?"
and get to a concrete price/budget/approver. A verbal "I'd try it" does not count.
**Current evidence:** None real. Simulated interviews split on where value lives (decisioning
vs logistics vs knowledge capture), which itself signals the buyer/product is not yet clear.

> ⚠️ Derived from SIMULATED interviews. Treat as the top hypothesis to test with real buyers.
