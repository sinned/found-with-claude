## Opportunity Areas

### 1. Sales Coaching Intelligence — Score: 13/15
**Pain: 5/5 | Underservice: 4/5 | Founder fit: 4/5**

**What the problem is:**
Sales managers at Series A-C SaaS companies spend 70% of their "coaching" time in
Salesforce, not with their reps. When they do coach, it is reactive (deal went wrong)
rather than proactive (here is what I see coming). The signals for proactive coaching
exist in the CRM — they are just never surfaced as actionable insights for managers.

**What people do today:**
Manual deal reviews once a week. A manager pulls Salesforce reports and tries to
pattern-match across 8-12 reps. Usually misses the early signals. Coaching happens
after the quarter has gone wrong, not before.

**The founder's edge:**
Priya has run RevOps at 3 Series B companies. She knows exactly which CRM signals
predict quota miss 6 weeks before the quarter ends. No one has built for managers
who need those signals — tools are built for VPs, not front-line managers.

**Key question to answer:**
Do sales managers have budget authority for tools, or does every purchase go through
VP Sales or RevOps? If managers cannot buy, the distribution becomes hard.

---

### 2. Quota Planning Automation — Score: 11/15
**Pain: 4/5 | Underservice: 4/5 | Founder fit: 3/5**

**What the problem is:**
Quota planning for 20+ reps is a quarterly process that takes RevOps 3-4 weeks
and produces quotas that everyone knows are wrong by month 2. The inputs are
in 5 different spreadsheets and no one agrees on the model.

**What people do today:**
Custom spreadsheet models, usually maintained by 1 person in RevOps who is the
only one who understands it. Takes forever to update when reps join, leave, or
change territories. Results in quotas that feel arbitrary to the sales team.

**The founder's edge:**
Priya has built quota models for 3 companies. She knows the inputs, the edge
cases, and the political dynamics. But this problem is more technical than it
appears — the data integration is hard and the TAM may be smaller than it looks.

**Key question to answer:**
Is the pain primarily in building the model or in getting buy-in on the output?
These require very different products.

---

### 3. RevOps to Sales Handoff — Score: 9/15
**Pain: 3/5 | Underservice: 3/5 | Founder fit: 3/5**

**What the problem is:**
When RevOps builds a new process or reporting framework, it takes months to get
sales to actually use it. The reps do not trust the data, do not understand the
process, and default to their own spreadsheets.

**What people do today:**
Training sessions, Confluence docs, Slack announcements. None of it sticks.

**The founder's edge:**
Priya has experienced this, but so has every RevOps person. The insight here is
not unique enough to give her a clear edge.

**Key question to answer:**
Is this a product problem or a change management problem? If it is change management,
the product is a training tool, which is a very different market.

## What to do next

**If you pursue #1:** Talk to 5 sales managers (not VPs, not RevOps — front-line
managers with 6-10 direct reports) at Series B SaaS companies. Ask them: "Walk me
through the last time you gave a rep a proactive coaching conversation before a
deal went wrong. What triggered you to do it?" If they cannot name a specific
trigger, the behavior is not happening — which means the market exists.

**If you are unsure between #1 and #2:** The deciding factor is distribution. Do
sales managers buy tools themselves? If yes, #1 has a faster path to revenue. If
no, #2 may have cleaner budget access through RevOps.

**Before talking to customers:** Have a point of view on what "proactive coaching"
means specifically. Not "AI-powered insights" — but "here are the 3 specific signals
that, if you saw them 6 weeks out, you would change your coaching conversation."
This specificity will make your customer discovery interviews 10x more productive.
